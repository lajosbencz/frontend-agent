# Dialing In

Dialing in is the process of tuning your grind so a specific coffee tastes its best. Espresso comes down to three numbers: **dose** (dry grounds in), **yield** (liquid espresso out), and **time** (how long the shot runs). You fix the first two and adjust grind until the third lands and the taste balances. The golden rule: **change only one variable at a time**, and while dialing that variable is almost always grind size. For the why behind these numbers, read [espresso fundamentals](/brewcraft/docs/espresso-fundamentals).

## The numbered process

1. **Set your DOSE and leave it fixed.** Weigh your dry grounds — about 18g in an 18g basket. Whatever you pick, keep it identical for every shot while dialing. Changing dose mid-session moves the target and hides the effect of your grind changes. Use the [Brew Scale](/brewcraft/products/brew-scale) for a repeatable dose.
2. **Set your target YIELD by ratio.** Start at the standard **1:2** — 18g in gives 36g out. Put your cup on the [Brew Scale](/brewcraft/products/brew-scale) and weigh the liquid espresso, not the cup, not the crema volume. Yield is measured by mass, in grams.
3. **Pull a shot and TIME it.** Start the timer the moment the pump starts and stop when you hit 36g. Target roughly **25-30 seconds** to reach your yield.
4. **Taste the shot and read the time together.** Too fast (under ~20s) usually tastes sour, thin, and weak — the water rushed through and under-extracted, so grind **finer** to add resistance. Too slow (over ~35s) usually tastes bitter, harsh, and dry — the water dragged and over-extracted, so grind **coarser** to speed it up.
5. **Change ONLY the grind between shots.** Move one small step at a time. Grinders overshoot easily, so resist big jumps. Stepless grinders like the [Vortex](/brewcraft/products/vortex-grinder) and [Precision](/brewcraft/products/precision-grinder) let you nudge grind in tiny amounts, ideal for espresso; the stepped [Compact](/brewcraft/products/compact-grinder) moves in fixed clicks, so if one click runs too fast and the next too slow, split the difference with a small dose or ratio tweak. After each change, run a little coffee through to purge old grounds so you taste the new setting.
6. **Re-pull and repeat.** Keep looping steps 3-5 until the shot both lands near 25-30s **and** tastes balanced — neither sour nor bitter. Time alone is not the goal; taste is the judge, and time is your guide toward it.
7. **Confirm you are dialed in.** You are done when you can repeat the same shot three times in a row with consistent time, yield, and taste. Write the setting down.

## Fresh beans matter

Every bean, roast, and even bag behaves differently, so a setting that worked last week may need adjusting today. Coffee is at its best roughly 5 to 21 days after roast. Very fresh beans (under ~4 days) can gush and channel from excess CO2. **A new bag always needs re-dialing** — start from your last known setting and re-run the loop.

## When taste and time disagree

If a shot is somehow both sour and bitter, or the time is right but it still tastes wrong, the problem is usually puck prep, not grind — head to [puck prep](/brewcraft/docs/puck-prep) and [troubleshooting](/brewcraft/docs/troubleshooting) before chasing the grinder further.
